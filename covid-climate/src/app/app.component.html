Influence of different Sectors

You might wonder which sectors were responsible for these emissions. Take a look at the sectors here:

  • {{sectorNames[i]}}

This breakdown clearly shows that a whole range of sectors contribute to the amount of global emissions. The energy sector is primarily made up of the energy used for electricity, heat and transport. Road transport emissions result from passenger vehicles such as cars and buses, as well as from trucks transporting goods. The industry sector represents CO2 emissions resulting from industrial processes, and the residential sector shows the emissions from residential structures that mainly derive from the amount of energy they consume. Finally, the domestic aviation sector summarizes the CO2 emissions caused by domestic flights in the selected country. By being aware of the impact of each sector on our total CO2 emissions figure, we can better understand which areas need to - and can - be targeted, in order to effectively reduce our carbon footprint.

Achieving the climate goals

We have seen that the Corona pandemic ensured that significantly less CO2 was emitted worldwide. However, the question remains, what good it has done to our climate. In the foreseeable future, the regulations will be loosened, and eventually lifted altogether. But what if - as of today - we would simply continue with the containment measures? Could these measures also be used to help us meet our climate goals? We set up two prognosis lines, that give insight into just that:

  • Continuation with regulations
  • Continuation without regulations

The y-axis depicts the amount of CO2 emissions per day, while the x-axis shows the years. The white graph represents the global CO2 of an average day from 1750 to 2020. From there, our prognosis graphs begin. The data for the prognosis for continuing without regulations, was calculated by the U.S. Energy Information Administration [1]. Based on this data, we calculated a forecast for continuing with regulations by mapping this years percentage decrease in CO2 emissions to the data from [1]. This of course means a decrease in emissions, but what could be the impacts on climate warming if we continued with the regulations experienced 2020 in all the upcoming years? According to the Mercator Research Institute on Global Commons and Climate Change (MCC) [2] the atmosphere can absorb roughly 1042 gigatons of CO2 from the beginning of 2021 on until the global temperature will be 2°C more than pre industrial levels. The two vertical, dashed lines are showing in which year the remaining CO2 budget will be depleted, with and without continuing regulations.
